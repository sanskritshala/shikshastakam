    Structural beauty

    In the first verse, the first wonderful effects are explained and the author reveals the mystery at the very end that all these wonderful effects are due to Sri Krishna sankirtanam. To depict the mood of victory of sankritanam, the structure of the verse is very joyful. Every compound word ends with an action noun, creating a sense of movement in activity.

    Structural beauty

    In the second verse, grammar is very complicated, where rarely occurring verbs are employed. The grammar structure is very formal to depict the mood of awe, reverence, or a sense of distance. Through this structure, the author wants to convey that you are great and I am very fallen. Whenever relation is formal (court proceedings, research talks, etc), very formal language is used. However, in the case of an intimate relationship between lovers, simple language is used.

Auchitya school

Basics

  • Kshemendra introduced the concept of auchitya in his work Auchitya Vichar Charcha.
  • Auchitya means appropriateness and regulates all aspects of Natya Shastra.
  • Auchitya aids writers in delivering their ideas effectively.
  • Categorized into several classes by Kshemendra: Pada, Vakya, Prabhandhanartha, Guna, Alankara, Rasa.

In conclusion, auchitya is an essential tool for writers, enabling them to compose beautiful poetry by using the elements of poetry in an appropriate manner.

Analysis

  • Shikshashtakam is an exemplary composition that demonstrates auchitya.
  • Uses Vipralamba Sringar rasa, Vaidarbhi reeti, and Madhurya guna perfectly.
  • Alankaras used in an appropriate manner without overwhelming the expression of the rasa.
  • Rasa remains consistent and intensifies gradually throughout the composition.
  • Reaches its pinnacle in the last verse.
  • Uniformity in the rasa's expression contributes to the effectiveness of the composition.
  • Evokes the desired emotional response effectively.

Vakrokti school

Basics

  • Kuntaka's Vakrokti school emphasizes the use of oblique expressions in poetry
  • Vakroti Jivika lays out the principles and classifies the levels of expression into six categories
  • The six categories are: phonetic figurativeness, lexical figurativeness, sentential figurativeness, contextual figurativeness, sectional figurativeness, and compositional figurativeness.
  • Vakrokti is considered the soul of poetry, and the only embellishment possible to the word and its meaning
  • Mastery of the six levels of expression is fundamental to creating effective poetry in the Vakroti school
  • Use of Vakrokti is essential to creating effective poetry

Analysis

  • The analysis focuses on the use of oblique expressions in Shikshashtakam.
  • Six categories of oblique expressions are identified in the poem.
  • The first category is phonetic figurativeness, which enhances the poetic sound using anupras alankar.
  • The second category is lexical figurativeness, which uses Rupak alankara at the level of individual words.
  • The third category is sentential figurativeness, which uses arthalankaras at the level of sentences.
  • The fourth category is contextual figurativeness, which relies on the poem's context and uses Rupaka alankaras to create a garland-like effect.
  • The fifth category is sectional figurativeness, which involves the twist in the chapter arrangement of the poetry.
  • The sixth category is compositional figurativeness, which involves understanding the oblique meaning by relying on the complete composition.
  • Shikshatakam uses all six categories of oblique expressions, demonstrating the author's mastery of poetic devices and his ability to convey complex spiritual ideas in a nuanced and layered manner.

Rasa school

Basics

  • Rasa school of Indian aesthetics emphasizes emotional impact of poetry on the reader or audience
  • Bharata Muni, author of Natyashastra, is most influential thinker associated with this school
  • Rasa theory posits that rasa is the ultimate emotional pleasure that can be derived from a work of art
  • Rasa is the heightened emotional response to the text
  • Bharata classified various emotions into eight different rasas: Shringara, Hasya, Karuna, Raudra, Veera, Bhayanaka, Bibhatsa, and Adbhuta
  • Other important figures associated with this school include Abhinavagupta, Anandavardhana, and Jagannatha Pandita
  • Formula for arousal of rasa presented in Natyashastra involves combination of vibhava, anubhava, and vyabhichari bhava.
  • Vibhava refers to stimulants of emotions, anubhava represents physical responses that accompany emotional reactions, and vyabhichari bhava denotes transitory emotions

Analysis

  • The author assumes the mood of a devotee and reciprocates with his beloved Lord Krishna
  • Author employs several words to indicate vibhav as Lord Krishna, including śrī-kṛṣṇa-saṅkīrtanam, nāmnām, hariḥ, nanda-tanuja, tava nāma-grahaṇe, govinda, and mat-prāṇa-nāthaḥ
  • Author's intense feelings of separation serve as anubhava, indicated by words such as nānurāgaḥ, a-darśanān, govinda-viraheṇa, and mat-prāṇa-nāthaḥ
  • Rasa evoked in composition is vipralamba sringar, characterized by intense feelings of separation and longing
  • Author effectively employs vibhavas and anubhavas to evoke rasa in Indian classical literature.

Reeti school

Basics

  • The concept of Reeti in poetry, also known as Marga or Samghatana, which is considered the soul of poetry by scholar Vamana.
  • There are three Reetis: Vaidarbhee, Gaudee, and Panchalee, named after the geographical areas where poets constructed in these styles.
  • The deterministic elements of Reeti include letters, length and quantity of compounds, and the complexity or simplicity of the composition.
  • Vaidarbhee style is the most delightful, consisting of all ten gunas explained by Vamana, and has a medium amount of samasas.
  • Gaudee style lacks Sukumarata and Madhurya gunas and has long compounds and complex diction dominated by Oja and Kanti gunas.
  • Panchalee Reeti contains the compositions which do not lie in Vaidarbhee and Gaudee.
  • Vamana describes ten qualities of sound and ten qualities of meaning, which are compressed into three gunas.
  • Proper deployment of the elements of Reeti can significantly enhance the impact of poetry on the reader or listener.

Analysis

  • The Shikshashtakam is a composition that embodies the Madhurya guna and Vaidarbhi reeti.
  • The usage of soft consonants and restricted joint consonants contributes to the simple composition of the Shikshashtakam.
  • The limited occurrence of long compounds in the Shikshashtakam is an aspect of Vaidarbhi reeti.
  • The rasa of the kavya is vipralambha srinagar, which is aptly brought out through the use of Vaidarbhi reeti and madhurya guna.
  • Choosing the appropriate reeti is crucial in conveying the intended rasa. Using any other reeti would have led to a rasa dosha.

Dhvani school

Basics

  • The Dhvani school is a prominent school of Indian poetics associated with Anandavardhana and other significant figures such as Abhinavagupta, Kuntaka, and Mahimabhatta.
  • The essence of poetry according to the Dhvani school lies in suggestion or Dhvani, which alludes to the verbal, hidden, and profound aspects of poetry.
  • The Dhvani school distinguishes three categories of meaning in poetry: (1) Vacyartha or literal meaning, (2) Lakshartha or indirect meaning, and (3) Vyangartha or poetic/metaphysical meaning.
  • Dhvani is divided into three broad categories: (1) Vastu-Dhvani, (2) Alankar-Dhvani, and (3) Rasa-Dhvani.
  • Based on the degree of Dhvani, a poem is classified into three categories: (1) Uttama Kavya or best composition, (2) Madhyama Kavya or mediocre composition, and (3) Adhama Kavya or low-level composition.
  • In Uttama Kavya, Dhvani is present, evoking wonder in the reader.
  • In Madhyama Kavya, Vyangartha is present, but it does not evoke wonder compared to Vacyartha, while in Adhama Kavya, Dhvani is absent.

Analysis

  • The verses are organized into 3 possible themes or dhvanis.
  • The first Dhvani deals with the sambandha, abhideya, and prayojana of a devotee.
  • The second Dhvani presents the higher stages of devotional service.
  • The third Dhvani is the biography of the author.
    • The author's proficiency in Sanskrit grammar and poetry is conveyed in the initial verse.
    • The author's victorious preaching of the message of saṅkīrtanam is illustrated in the biography.
    • The author's egalitarian nature and adherence to the principle of humility are evident in the verses.
    • The last three verses reflect the author's mood of separation in the last 24 years of his life.
  • The author's teachings in the Siksastakam are a reflection of his own life experiences and practices.

Is Shikshastakam an Uttama Kavya?

  • Uttama Kavya requires the presence of Dhvani that evokes wonder in the reader.
  • Dhvani is of three types: Vastu-Dhvani, Alankar-Dhvani, and Rasa-Dhvani.
  • Shikshastakam explores the sambandha, abhideya, and prayojana of a devotee, gradations of devotional service, and the biography of the author in the context of Vastu-Dhvani.
  • Shikshastakam employs a range of figures of speech such as metaphors, similes, and other literary devices to express its emotional essence (rasa) without hindrance in the context of Alankar-Dhvani.
  • Shikshastakam is an exemplar of Rasa-Dhvani as it conveys the feeling of separation from the divine and generates a deep emotional response in the reader.
  • In summary, Shikshastakam is the Uttama Kavya due to the presence of Vastu-Dhvani, Alankar-Dhvani, and Rasa-Dhvani .
